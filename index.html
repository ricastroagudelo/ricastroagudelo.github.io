About

A scholar with a journalist's ear and a curator's eye.

I grew up in Colombia and came to the study of memory through a long detour. Before arriving at UT Austin, I spent six years running the Language and Writing program at Universidad Externado de Colombia, programmed cultural events at the Centro Cultural Gabriel García Márquez in Bogotá, edited at Fondo de Cultura Económica, and wrote for Revista Arcadia and El País, where I interviewed writers like Fernando Vallejo, Junot Díaz, and Franco Moretti.

That earlier life shapes how I read now. I approach post-conflict texts as someone who has sat across a table from storytellers and learned to listen for what they can't quite say. My research asks how readers and viewers come to feel their way into the experiences of others, and what the limits of that feeling might be.

At UT Austin I also work at the Harry Ransom Center, where I've engaged with the papers of Gabriel García Márquez, Arthur Miller, Oscar Cásares, and Alfred A. Knopf, Inc., among others. I publish in English and Spanish and read Portuguese.

Research

Expanding the gray zone.

Dissertation

Expanding the Gray Zone: Empathy, Emotional Engagement and Collective Memory after War

Defended April 17, 2026 · PhD conferred May 8, 2026

In Evelio Rosero's novel Los Ejércitos, an old man hears what sounds like thunder. His body knows better. Years at the edge of Colombia's conflict have taught him what the sound actually is. In William Vega's film La Sirga, a young woman makes the same mistake, and her uncle corrects her: that is not thunder. My dissertation begins with this kind of bodily knowledge and asks how a novel, a film, or a photograph can pass it to a reader or viewer who has never lived anywhere near that war.

Cultural production from post-conflict Colombia and Peru does something that truth commissions and official archives cannot. It puts a reader or viewer inside the body of someone who lived through violence, making the war felt before it is explained. Working from cognitive cultural studies, and drawing in particular on Vittorio Gallese's theory of embodied simulation, Suzanne Keen on narrative empathy, and Alison Landsberg on prosthetic memory, I read novels by Rosero and Claudia Salazar Jiménez; nonfiction by former FARC combatants and the memoir of the Peruvian former child soldier Lurgio Gavilán; the films Días de Santiago (Josué Méndez) and La Sirga (Vega); and the photography of Jesús Abad Colorado, Federico Ríos, and the Yuyanapaq exhibition produced by Peru's Truth and Reconciliation Commission. The title borrows from Primo Levi: the gray zone is the moral territory that extreme violence creates, where victim and perpetrator blur. My argument is that cultural production widens the community of people who can feel its weight.

Co-chairs: Gabriela Polit Dueñas and Hannah Wojciehowski
Committee: Donna DeCesare, Jason Borge, Cory Reed

Teaching

Helping students read closely and write honestly.

I've been teaching language and writing for more than a decade, first in Bogotá and now in Austin. My classroom is built around close reading, careful revision, and the conviction that students learn to write by writing about things that matter to them. In literature courses, I ask students to treat texts as sites of genuine inquiry, not targets for decoding.

My teaching draws on my scholarly work: I want students to notice how stories, images, and testimony shape what they feel before they analyze what they think. Recent and proposed courses include:

Intermediate Spanish: Contemporary Latin American Culture

Language acquisition through literary texts, films, and journalism from across the region.

Memory and Violence in Latin American Literature

A course on testimonio, post-conflict fiction, and the cultural afterlives of political violence.

The Latin American Short Story

From Borges and Rulfo to Schweblin and Enríquez, a workshop-style introduction to the form.

Writing Latin America: Journalism and Literature

A craft-based course where reportage and fiction meet, informed by my years as a working journalist.

Curatorial & Archival

Working in the archives.

I'm a Curatorial Assistant and Graduate Research Assistant at the Harry Ransom Center at UT Austin, where I've moved through several roles since joining as a Career Pathway Fellow. Archival work has become a central part of my scholarly practice: the texture of a manuscript, a marked-up typescript, or a stack of correspondence tells a story that the published record never fully captures.

Collections I've worked with include:

  • Gabriel García Márquez Papers
  • Arthur Miller Papers
  • Oscar Cásares Papers
  • Alfred A. Knopf, Inc. Records
  • Susan Bergholz Literary Papers

Before UT Austin, I programmed cultural events and exhibitions at the Centro Cultural Gabriel García Márquez in Bogotá, a formative experience that still shapes how I think about bringing literature and visual culture to public audiences.
